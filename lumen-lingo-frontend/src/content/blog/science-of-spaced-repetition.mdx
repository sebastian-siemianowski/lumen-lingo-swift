You've probably experienced this: you study a list of vocabulary words, feel confident, then discover a week later that most of them have evaporated from memory. This isn't a personal failing — it's a fundamental feature of how human memory works, first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885.

The good news? Cognitive science has also discovered the antidote. It's called **spaced repetition**, and it's the most powerful learning technique that most people have never heard of.

## The Forgetting Curve

Ebbinghaus discovered that memory decay follows a predictable exponential curve. After learning something new, you forget roughly:

- **50% within one hour** without review
- **70% within 24 hours**
- **90% within one week**

This is the **forgetting curve**, and it applies to almost all new information — vocabulary, historical facts, scientific concepts, musical notation. The curve is steep and unforgiving.

But Ebbinghaus also discovered something remarkable: **each time you review information at the point of forgetting, the curve flattens**. The first review might keep a memory alive for two days. The second review extends it to a week. The third to a month. Eventually, the information becomes part of your long-term memory with minimal maintenance.

## How Spaced Repetition Works

Spaced repetition is elegantly simple in concept: **review information just before you're about to forget it**.

The challenge is knowing *when* that moment is — and that's where algorithms come in. Modern spaced repetition systems track your performance on each item and calculate the optimal review interval.

Here's a simplified model of how intervals grow:

| Review # | If Correct | If Incorrect |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1st | Review in 1 day | Review in 4 hours |
| 2nd | Review in 3 days | Reset to 1 day |
| 3rd | Review in 7 days | Reset to 3 days |
| 4th | Review in 14 days | Reset to 7 days |
| 5th | Review in 30 days | Reset to 14 days |

The intervals increase roughly exponentially for correct answers. A single incorrect answer compresses the interval, but doesn't start you over completely — the algorithm remembers your previous successes.

This isn't just theoretical — a 2019 meta-analysis of 29 studies found that spaced repetition produces **74% better long-term retention** compared to massed practice (cramming), with the effect size increasing over longer time periods.
</Callout>

Some vocabulary items interfere with each other. For example, Spanish learners often confuse *ser* and *estar* (both mean "to be") or *por* and *para* (both translate to "for"). ## The Neuroscience Behind It

Why does spacing work at the neural level? Current research points to several mechanisms:

### Reconsolidation

When you retrieve a memory, it temporarily becomes malleable — a process called **reconsolidation**. During this window, the memory can be strengthened with new connections. Spaced retrieval creates more reconsolidation events, each one strengthening the memory trace.

### Contextual Variation

When you review at different times and in different states, you create multiple retrieval pathways to the same memory. This is called **encoding variability** — the memory becomes accessible from more mental starting points, making it more robust and flexible.

### Desirable Difficulty

Psychologist Robert Bjork introduced the concept of **desirable difficulty** — the idea that learning tasks should be challenging enough to require effort, but not so hard that they cause failure. Spaced repetition naturally creates desirable difficulty by testing you right at the edge of forgetting.

## Making Spaced Repetition Work for You

Understanding the science is valuable, but the real power comes from consistent practice. Here's how to get the most from spaced repetition:

### Trust the Algorithm

The hardest part of spaced repetition is trusting the schedule. You might feel like you should review a word more often, or that reviewing "easy" words is a waste of time. Resist this urge — the algorithm has access to patterns your intuition can't detect.

### Never Skip a Day

The spacing effect depends on timely reviews. Missing a day creates a cascade — overdue items pile up, and the algorithm's predictions become less accurate. Even a five-minute session on a busy day maintains the schedule.

### Don't Cram Before Tests

If you have a language exam, your instinct might be to do a massive review session the night before. This actually undermines spaced repetition by resetting intervals prematurely. Trust that your steady daily practice has prepared you better than any cram session could.
